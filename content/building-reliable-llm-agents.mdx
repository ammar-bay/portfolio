When I started building [VidSpotAI](https://vidspotai.com), I had a naive mental model of an "AI agent": one big prompt, one model, one shot. That model breaks the moment real users show up. Generations fail, costs spike, and "mostly works" becomes a support nightmare.

What actually held up in production was treating the agent less like a chatbot and more like a small, observable pipeline.

## Plan, then execute

The single biggest reliability win was separating **planning** from **execution**.

- A cheap, fast router (Haiku) classifies intent and routes work — most requests never need the expensive path.
- A planner decomposes the task into a structured storyboard before any generation happens.
- An executor drives generation against that plan, one step at a time.

Because the plan is structured data (not free text), every later stage can reason about it, retry it, or repair it.

## Critique before you ship

Generation is non-deterministic, so I never trust a single result. Each output runs through:

1. **Dual critics** that score the result against the plan.
2. A **quality gate** with score bands, retry-budget caps, and optional human-approval steps.
3. A **self-healing repair engine** that degrades or regenerates weak steps instead of failing the whole job.

This is the same idea as an `LLM-as-judge` eval harness — except it runs in production, on every request, with a cost budget attached.

## Make everything observable

The part nobody tells you: agents are only as good as your ability to see what they did. Per-stage trace and timing instrumentation, plus per-stage cost tracking, turned "why did this fail?" from a guessing game into a lookup.

## Takeaways

- Structure the plan as data, not prose.
- Route cheap by default; spend tokens only where they matter.
- Verify with critics + a quality gate — assume any single generation can be wrong.
- Instrument every stage. You can't fix what you can't see.
